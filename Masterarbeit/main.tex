\chapter{Introduction}
Financial markets are highly complex systems that are influenced by human behaviours and economic events. For decades, data scientists and economists have attempted to predict and explain financial time series, but it still remains a big challenge. Traditionally, autoregressive models or technical indicators were used to achieve this goal. While this approach created a mathematical and statistical foundation, it falls short when confronted with unstructured data like news headlines or investor sentiment.

With the rapid advances in artificial intelligence (AI), especially in natural language processing (NLP), new ways for understanding those unstructured texts have been found. For example, transformer models can extract semantic information from text. This information could be used to engineer new features that help explain variance in the movement of stock market indices like the S\&P500 or the MSCI World. 

The goal of this master thesis, therefore, is to investigate if a stochastic forecasting approach can be improved by adding sentiment-based features extracted from news headlines and/or social media posts. This feature can be understood as an indicator (usually ranged between -1 and 1), that tries to reflect the emotions of investors towards the current economy. In the scope of a single headline it captures how optimistic (bullish) or pessimistic (bearish) the context appears.

For analysing the quantitative benefit of this feature multiple stochastic and complex maschine learning models will be compared against baselines in regression and classification tasks. The evaluation is going to be performed by adding and removing the sentiment column from a given feature matrix to see if the prediction performance increases, flattens or decreases. To validate the outcome expanding window cross validation is used. Lastly to analyse the performance of both tasks common metrics like Mean Percentage Error (MAP) or F1-Scores are utilized.

This investigation contributes to an ongoing debate about the efficient market hypothesis.

\chapter{Literature Review}
At the core of financial market prediction lies the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) \cite{fama1965behavior}, that states that every available information is immediatly priced in. Resulting in an environment in which consistent outpeforming by leveraging current and past information is not possible. Specifically, this model can be broken down into three forms. The weak form includes past price data, while the semi-strong form also includes all other publicly available information like financial news headlines. The strong form states that every information be it public or insider knowledge is also immediatly priced in \cite{emh1970}. While this model is a reasonable foundation some other researchers see it very critical. For example the EMH assumes that investors are completely rational and can comprehend all information which is unrealistic \cite{sul2017trading}. A good example for this point gives \cite{lawrence1997using} which statesthat the market crash of 1987 was due to overwhelming investors fear rather than rational informative decisions. An alternative market model called Gradient Information Flow (GIF) was introduced in \cite{hong1999unified}. In short the GIF model can be explained as seeing investors in two groups. One group the "news watchers" only focus on fundamental news and "momentum traders" which focus only on past price data with both failing to see the whole picture. This results in market anomalies where price is not alway reasonable.

To capture the chaotic price changes and behavioral anomalies, researchers have shifted from traditional technical indicators often seen as subjective and unreliable \cite{lawrence1997using} to Neural Networks (NNs). Neural networks can helpt to analyse patterns behin the chaotic movement of the stock market \cite{ZHANG201860} or to analyse unstructured information like financial news. Especially with the advances in Natural Language Processing (NLP) there are some new opportunities for analysing textural data. New models like FinBERT \cite{araci2019finbert} allow for a more sufficticated sentiment analysis opposed by the traditional ways with lexicons \cite{loughran2011liability}. One empirical study \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742} utilized both financial news and social media data to gauge this sentiment. Combining it with technical indicators yielded improved performance on forex and crypto prices. Similarly, social media platforms like Twitter and StockTwits have been mined for real time mood indicators improving prediction accuracy for major tech stocks by capturing same-day sentiment scores \cite{9175549,sul2017trading}. In predicitng market direction (up, down) \cite{CHOI2024121589,khan2022stock} have used sentiment features to achieve a stated prediction accuracy of around 80\% with advanced ensemble methods like Random Forest or Gradient Boosting.

Despite the high performance claims, the ability to consistently and profitable beat the EMH remains an ongoing debate. \cite{ZHANG201860} argues that there is no guarantee that sentiment does not include noise and misleading information. This skepticism is also supported by \cite{jrfm18080412}, who analyzed 1.86 million headlines using mulitple sentiment models (VADER, TextBlob, FinBERT). They conclueded that explicit sentiment scores lack robust predictive power in regression and classification tasks, failing to reject the Efficient Market Hypothesis.

This ongoing debate highlights following important question: where is sentiment actually effective? While previous studies \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742,9175549} shows benefit on single equities or specific currency pairs, the prediction of broad market indicies remains a unique challenge. This paper will focus on predicting the S\&P500 index to test if sentiment can effectively reflect braod macroeconomic moods or if it merely introduces noise. By doing so, this study adds a new data point to the EMH and GIF discussion, testing the limits and boundaries of market efficiency.

\chapter{Methodology}
\section{Sentiment Models}
For the sentiment classification task two different models are used and compared: FinBERT and VADER. The reason for this decision is to compare which of the models performs better in sentiment analysis. Doing so will show how important contextual understanding of transformers are when it comes to sentiment classification of short texts like news headlines.

\subsection{VADER}
Vader is a traditional lexicon and rule based sentiment model \cite{hutto2014vader}. It was developed to analyse short sentences like social media posts, product reviews, or short editorials in news papers. It assignes each word a predefined polarity score and adjusts the values based on heuristical rules. Negations, Intensifiers (extremly, marginally, ...), Capitalisation are some examples for those rules. They are used to get more contextual understanding. 

The final output of the model is called Compound-Score and is calculated by summing all polarity values and normalize:

$$s_{compound} = \frac{\sum v_i}{\sqrt{\left(\sum v_i\right)^2 + \alpha}}$$

Where $v_i$ are the adjusted polarity scores and $\alpha$ a normalization constant (default value $\alpha = 15$), which acts as sensitivity dial. As final output this formula guarantees a value between -1 (negative sentiment) and 1 (positive sentiment).

\subsection{FinBERT}
FinBERT is a state of the art sentiment model which in contrast uses a transformer architecture instead of a lexicon approach \cite{araci2019finbert}. It is a fine tuned version of the famous BERT model from Google \cite{devlin2019bert} and was trained on a large corpus of financial text. Because of its architecture and fine tuning it exceeds in the task of understanding semantic texts in the financial domain. At the end of the model architecture a dense layer for classification was added. The output is passed through a softmax function to obtain the probability distribution of the three senitment labels (positive, negative, neutral).

$$P(y|x) = \text{softmax}(W \cdot h_{\text{[CLS]}} + b)$$

Where P(y|x) stands for the conditional probability, $h_{\text{[CLS]}}$ for the CLS Token (compressed representation vector of the sentence) and W, b for the learned parameters of the classification layer.

\section{Impact Scoring}
Financial markets create a great volume of textual data. However, the distribution of important information is highly skewed. Most headlines are "noise" (repetitive updates, foreign news, minor price fluctuations), which can dilute the predictive power of the sentiment score. For this reason, a novel denoising method that uses large language models (LLMs) to differentiate between important headlines and noise is introduced.

The main component of this filtering process is Llama 3.1 Instruct (8B). To increase the throughput and memory efficiency, the model was implemented using a 6-bit quantization (.gguf format). The resulting memory footprint is approximately 6.85 GB, which enables local inference on consumer-grade hardware. Because this model is not fine-tuned a prompt had to be engineered. A self-annotated dataset of ~70 classified headlines served as a benchmark to compare different prompts. This is the final version that is used in further experiments:

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{PICs/Impact_Scoring_Classification_Prompt.png}
    \caption{Llama 3.1 Instruct (8B) prompt to classify news headlines}
    \label{fig:myimag13}
\end{figure}

Headlines are categorized in four classes (A,B,C,D) based on their predictive power on future S\&P500 returns. These labels are then transformed into a numerical number (A->1, B-> 0.66, C->0.33, D->0) called impact score.

The final output of this procedure is a denoised weighted sentiment score. Rather than treating every sentiment score equally, we apply the Impact Score as an additional transformation. This ensures that a positive sentiment on a minor headline (Category C) contributes less to the overall trend than a positive sentiment on a major headline (Category A). The calculation is defined as:

$$S_{w} = S \times I$$

Where:$S_w$ is the final weighted sentiment.
$S$ is the raw sentiment score (ranging from -1 to 1).
$I$ is the LLM-derived impact score (0 to 1).

\subsection{Regression}
The regression task aims to test how well a model can predict exact numbers of future returns. Specifically, the models attempt to predict the price percentage change $y_{t+1}$ based on the feature sets defined in Table \ref{tab:feature_configuration_setups}. This approach will show how well the model can learn to predict not only the direction of the price change but also the magnitude.

The target variable for the regression models is percentage change of the SP500 returns at time $t+1$.

$$R_t = \frac{P_t - P_{t-1}}{P_{t-1}}$$

% Todo fact check again
\begin{quote}
\small \textit{Note: Log returns may be preferred for other financial forecasting tasks \cite{fama1965behavior}. In this work however, the "simple" percentage change is sufficient because price change isn't used additively and variability is accounted for by the VIX index. The only arguable benefit of log returns would be that it is symmetric (a drop by 5\% followed by a 5\% increase is not zeroing out with normal percentage change). After testing both targets, the performance difference is negligible.}
\end{quote}

To evaluate the benefit of each forecaster, a baseline model was implemented that simply predicts the mean price change of the training set. This serve as a benchmark to evaluate if any sentiment or non-sentiment provides a gain in performance.

$$\bar{y} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i$$

To compare the different forcasting models across the different feature sets the following loss function and metrics are utilized:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean Squared Error (MSE)}: Measures the average suqared difference between the target and the predicted value, penalizing large outlierts more heavily. Allthough used as a loss function for regression models like multiple linear regression (MLR), for the final evaluation pipeline MSE is excluded because it is not suited for small numbers like percentage changes. Instead Mean Absolute Error and Root Mean Squared Error are used for the final comparison.
    \item \textbf{Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)}: Represents the square root of the MSE. It helps to transform the magnitude of the error back into the units of the target variable, making it easier to interpret.
    \item \textbf{Mean Absolute Error}: Calculates the average of the absolute differences between target and prediction. In contrast to MSE and RMSE it does not square errors, making it less sensitive against outliers.
    \item \textbf{R2}: Measures the proportion of variance in the target variable that can be explained by the feature set. If the value is around 0 it means that the model is not better than the baseline (predicting always the mean of the train target distribution).
\end{itemize}

To achieve a robust analysis of the regression task, a mix of traditional and advanced machine learning models is used. Using both statistical baselines and more complex recurrent neural networks provides a better comparison than relying on only one architecture. Therefore, the following models will be evaluated:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ARMA(X) (Auto-Regressive Moving Average)}: A statistical model for forecasting time series data. It mixes a Auto-Regressive(AR) component, which uses previous target variables and a Moving Average(MA) component, which uses the models past forecasting errors. To include additional inputs like sentiment a extended version called ARMAX is used (the X comes from ARMA with eXogenous variables).
    \item \textbf{LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)}: An advanced RNN (Recurrent Neural Network) architecture that uses three distinct gates (input, output and forget) to improve long-term dependency learning. In financial forcasting, LSTM is often used and among the most performant models. %Quellen hier zitieren
    \item \textbf{GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit)}: A type of RNN that is designed to efficiently handle sequential data. Compared to the LSTM model it has only two gates (update, reset) to mitigate for the vanishing gradient problem. Because of its architecture it is more lightweight than a LSTM but often achieve comparable performance.
    \item \textbf{MLR (Multiple Linear Regression)}: A fundamental statistical method that models the linear relationship between the target variable and two or more explanatory features. In this evaluation, MLR serves as a highly interpretable, linear baseline model to contrast against the more complex, non-linear deep learning approaches (GRU and LSTM).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Classification}
In contrast to the regression task, the classification task shifts the focus away from predicting exact magnitudes to forecaste the binary direction of market movements. Specifically, this evaluation is going to analyse if the sentiment feature is benefitial when it comes to accuratly classifying the direction of the market (up or down).

The target variable for the classification task is the binary direction of the market $Y_{t+1}$. Specifically, wethever the S\&P500 percentage change stated in the regression section is positive or flat/negative. The formular is as follows:

$$Y_{t+1} = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if } R_{t+1} > 0 \\ 0, & \text{if } R_{t+1} \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

For the binary classificaton problem a random guessing model would yield an accuracy of around 50 percent. Howerever, the markets history shows that there is a class imbalance in favor of upward movements. Therefore the baseline will be a classification model that always predicts the majority class, resulting in a more robust and real-world benchmark for comparison.

To evaluate the performance and compare classification models across different feature sets the following metrics are utilized:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy}: Measures the overall porportion of right guesses. Accuracy is an important benchmark to understand model performance, however it sometimes does not show the complete picture. The model could learn to predict always up and yield a good performance alltough not optimal. For this reason other metrics from the confusion matrix are necesarry.
    \item \textbf{Precision}: Calculates the ratio of true positive predictions to all positive predictions made by the model. In the context of financial forecasting, precision is critical because it indicates how reliable a model is when it signals an upward market movement. This metric is important if the goal is to minimize wrong buy signals.
    \item \textbf{Recall}: Measures the ratio of true positive predictions to all actual positive cases. A model with high recall (sensitivity) missed very few upward cases (investment opportunities).
    \item \textbf{F1-Score}: Represents the harmonic mean of precision and recall. Giving a good overview over the tradeoff between recall and precision. Specifically the balance between recalling all positive cases and actually beeing correct with the positive guesses.
\end{itemize}

To conduct a robust evaluation of the classification task, the analysis compares a traditional statistical method against an advanced ensemble learning technique. The following models will be evaluated:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multiple Logistic Regression (MLogR)}: The classification counterpart to Multiple Linear Regression. It models the probability of the market moving upward using a logistic function based on the explanatory features. In this evaluation, MLogR serves as a highly interpretable, linear baseline for the classification task, providing a benchmark to assess whether more complex, non-linear relationships exist in the data.
    \item \textbf{XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting)}: A highly performant, tree-based ensemble learning algorithm. XGBoost builds multiple decision trees sequentially, with each new tree attempting to correct the residual errors of the previous ones. It is robust to outliers, handles non-linear relationships effectively, and is widely regarded as a state-of-the-art model for tabular financial data classification.
\end{itemize}

\section{Validation}
Because financial forecasting is based on timeseries based market data we cannot use normal cross validation like k-fold. The reason is that if we shuffle datapoints randomly we break the causality of time (yesterday can not happend before tomorrow). Therefore a different approach hast to be used. There are two main ways on how to cross validate the evaluation models:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rolling Window}: In this approach the size of the traing data is fixed. After finishing a validation step, new data becomes part of the next train set while the old data is droped. This leads to a sliding window over the whole data with multiple combinations of train and test sets.
    \item \textbf{Expanding Window}: In this appraoch the size of the training data is growing with each validation step. After each round new obeservations are included in the train set. Therefore the model learns from the maximum available historical context.
\end{itemize}

In this work the Expanding Window strategy is used to incooperate the whole train set in the hope that the model can achieve better generalization. Because this paper evaluates varying forecast horizons, the standard implementation of expanding window was adapted to prevent look-ahead bias. Specifically, days that are used to calculate the target are excluded by a gap between train and test set. For example, if the target is a 3-day percentage change utilizing $y_{t-1}$, $y_{t-2}$, and $y_{t-3}$. Non of 3 days may be included in the train set. This assures a strict boundary for the models learning data, with no information leaks into the future.
