\chapter{Introduction}
Financial markets are highly complex systems that are influenced by human behaviours and economic events. For decades, data scientists and economists have attempted to predict and explain financial time series, but it still remains a big challenge. Traditionally, autoregressive models or technical indicators were used to achieve this goal. While this approach created a mathematical and statistical foundation, it falls short when confronted with unstructured data like news headlines or investor sentiment.

With the rapid advances in artificial intelligence (AI), especially in natural language processing (NLP), new ways for understanding those unstructured texts have been found. For example, transformer models can extract semantic information from text. This information could be used to engineer new features that help explain variance in the movement of stock market indices like the S\&P500 or the MSCI World. 

The goal of this master thesis, therefore, is to investigate if a stochastic forecasting approach can be improved by adding sentiment-based features extracted from news headlines and/or social media posts. This feature can be understood as an indicator (usually ranged between -1 and 1), that tries to reflect the emotions of investors towards the current economy. In the scope of a single headline it captures how optimistic (bullish) or pessimistic (bearish) the context appears.

As a proof of concept (PoC), the work will analyse an ARMA model with and without sentiment integration. Later, more complex AI models – ranging from XGBoost to recurrent neural networks (RNNs), long short-term memory networks (LSTMs), and state-of-the-art transformer models like GPT – 5 may be evaluated and benchmarked. To analyse performance, metrics such as Mean Percentage Error (MPE), Accuracy, and F1-Score will be used.

This investigation contributes to an ongoing debate about the efficient market hypothesis, which states that new information is integrated into prices almost instantly, rendering the information useless for predicting future movements.

\chapter{Motivation}
Despite decades-long research in terms of stock predictions, the forecasting of short-term movements remains a challenge. Traditional methods depend solely on previous price movements and technical indicators. However, the influential factors that move the market go far beyond this information. News, Social Media, politics, macro-economic events are examples that could contribute to changes in the stock market as well.

Current studies \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742} increasingly work with the analysis of media texts to achieve additional prediction-accuracy, though, the quantitative value of these features is debated. Additionally, the interaction between technical indicators and sentiment features — and their impact on model performance across different AI architectures — remains an open question.

\chapter{Literature Review}
The idea of combining market sentiment with stochastic and technical indicators is not new. Many studies have already done research on it \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742} \cite{10380556} \cite{9175549}. The combination of sentiment and well known indicators like ADI (divergences between the price and the volume), volume, EMA (weighted moving average) are used in \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742} to predict the price changes in Forex (Foreign exchange markets), with good performance. Similarly \cite{9175549} tried using daily aggregated sentiment and stock-data (isn´t further explained) to predict price changes of certain companies like Apple, Amazon or Microsoft. Both works have proven a correlation between stock price and sentiment, which led to increased model performance. Also notable is that \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742} stated that specifically combining both technical indicators and sentiment led to the most performant model, hinting possible feature synergies or complementations.

However, newspaper headlines are not the only reliable source, social media posts (e.g. twitter or social hubs) can also be utilized to create sentiment features. For example \cite{CHOI2024121589} used properties like views, likes, dislikes, and keywords to predict price momentum (up, down). Other studies like \cite{khan2022stock} even go a step further by combining both news paper headlines with social media content to create more robust accuracy.

In comparison to others this study will focus not on single companies or foreign exchange markets but broad stock indices. One might argue that sentiment analysis is more effective for indices because it reflects more macroeconomic factors. However, the opposite could also be true. Headlines related to a specific company (not included by the index) may introduce noise into the aggregated sentiment score, potentially reducing predictive power. Comparing the outcome of this study with the related ones may give new insights on this topic.

\chapter{Methodology}
\section{Sentiment Models}
For the sentiment classifcation task two different models are used and compared. FinBERT and VADER. The reason for this decision is to compare which of the models performs better in sentiment analysis. Doing so will show how important contextual understanding of transformers are when it comes to sentiment classification of short texts like news headlines.

\subsection{VADER}
Vader is a traditional lexicon and rule based sentiment model \cite{hutto2014vader}. It was developed to analyse short sentences like social media posts, product reviews, or short editorials in news papers. It assignes each word a predefined polarity score and adjusts the values based on heuristical rules. Negations, Intensifiers (extremly, marginally, ...), Capitalisation are some examples for those rules. They are used to get more contextual understanding. 

The final output of the model is called Compound-Score and is calculated by summing all polarity values and normalize:

$$s_{compound} = \frac{\sum v_i}{\sqrt{\left(\sum v_i\right)^2 + \alpha}}$$

Where $v_i$ are the adjusted polarity scores and $\alpha$ a normalization constant (default value $\alpha = 15$), which acts as sensitivity dial. As final output this formula guarantees a value between -1 (negative sentiment) and 1 (positive sentiment).

\subsection{FinBERT}
FinBERT is a state of the art sentiment model which in contrast uses a transformer architecture instead of a lexicon approach \cite{araci2019finbert}. It is a fine tuned version of the famous BERT model from Google \cite{devlin2019bert} and was trained on a large corpus of financial text. Because of its architecture and fine tuning it exceeds in the task of understanding semantic texts in the financial domain. At the end of the model architecture a dense layer for classification was added. The output is passed through a softmax function to obtain the probability distribution of the three senitment labels (positive, negative, neutral).

$$P(y|x) = \text{softmax}(W \cdot h_{\text{[CLS]}} + b)$$

Where P(y|x) stands for the conditional probability, $h_{\text{[CLS]}}$ for the CLS Token (compressed representation vector of the sentence) and W, b for the learned parameters of the classification layer.
\section{Datasets}
The data for the news sentiment score is joined by a datapipeline that combine, preprocess and align multiple datasets into one corpus of headlines. The aggregation of multiple news sources over a broader time horizon increases data coverage and diversity. Therefore reducing potential biases coming from a specific news portal or timeframe. Resulting in a more generalized and reliable sentiment signal.

Following datasets are included in the pipeline:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Financial News Headlines Data} (Pham, \cite{pham_financial_news}): Kaggle hosted dataset that spans from December 2017 to July 2020. It contains about 53330 Headlines scraped from CNBC, the Guardian, and Reuters official websites.
    
    \item \textbf{Daily Financial News for 6000+ Stocks} (Aenlle, \cite{aenlle_financial_news}): Kaggle hosted dataset that covers 1.85 million news from 2009-2020. Covers many news portals like Seeking Alpha, Zacks, GuruFocus. The Timestamps are localized as UTC-4 (US/Eastern).
    
    % maybe move the potential error explanation into the appendix
    \item \textbf{FNSPID: Financial News Dataset in Time Series} (Dong et al., \cite{dong2024fnspid}): This dataset aggregates ~15.7 million news headlines from Nasdaq. The corpus offers a datapoints from 1999 through 2023. Allthough promissing, it appears to have wrong time convertions on some entries. For example Benzinga sourced headlines are localized as UTC-4, the corresponding timestamp in the dataset is localized as UTC-0 but converted in the wrong direction. This leads to potential data leakage if the article was published after 8 pm.
    
    \item \textbf{NIFTY: News-Informed Financial Trend Yield} (Saqur, \cite{raeidsaqur2024NiftyLM}): Originally a dataset designed for aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with financial market data. Contains headlines that are mostly about the indian/asian economy. In the context of this study, it is included as a benchmark to determine if there are performance variations compared to other datasets.
\end{itemize}

\section{Impact Scoring}
Financial markets generate a great volume of textual data. However, the distribution of important information is highly skewed. Most headlines are "noise" (repetitive updates, foreign news, minor price fluctuations), which can dilute the predictive power of sentiment analysis. Therefore this research introduces a novel denoising method that uses large language models (LLMs) to differntiate between important headlines and noise.

The main component of this filtering process is Llama 3.1 Instruct (8B). To increase the throughput and memory efficiency the model was implemented using a 6-bit quantization (.gguf format). The resulting memory foodprint is approximatly 6.85 GB, which enables local interference on consumer-grade hardware. Because this model is not finetuned a prompt had to be engineered. A self annotated dataset of ~70 classified headlines served as benchmark to compare different prompts. This is the final version that is used in further experiments:

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{PICs/Impact_Scoring_Classification_Prompt.png}
    \caption{Llama 3.1 Instruct (8B) prompt to classify news headlines}
    \label{fig:myimag13}
\end{figure}

Headlines are categorized in four classes (A,B,C,D) based on their predictive power on future S\&P500 returns. These labels are then transformed into a numerical number (A->1, B-> 0.66, C->0.33, D->0) called impact score.

The final output of this procedure is a denoised weighted sentiment score. Rather than treating every sentiment score equally, we apply the Impact Score as an additional transformation. This ensures that a positive sentiment on a minor headline (Category C) contributes less to the overall trend than a positive sentiment on a major headline (Category A). The calculation is defined as:

$$S_{w} = S \times I$$

Where:$S_w$ is the final weighted sentiment.
$S$ is the raw sentiment score (ranging from -1 to 1).
$I$ is the LLM-derived impact score (0 to 1).
