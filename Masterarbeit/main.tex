\outline{
    This research analyses the predictive value of financial news sentiment on the percentage price change of the S\&P500, serving as a new data point in the ongoing debate surrounding the semi-strong form of the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH). To quantify the benefit of sentiment features, a comprehensive data pipeline was implemented. It is able to combine multiple news headline datasets and extracts sentiment using both Large Language Model (LLM)-based and rule-based sentiment models. The resulting feature matrix was tested across both regression (predicting exact percentage price change) and classification (predicting future market direction) tasks on multiple evaluation models. The results suggest that in a fair, non-lookahead setup, outperforming baseline models was not successful. However, further analysis showed that there is a non-linear correlation between sentiment and price change volatility. Furthermore, utilizing LLMs as a denoising method to filter news headlines by importance demonstrated a small but consistent improvement in performance, suggesting a promising avenue for future research.
}

\chapter{Introduction}
Financial markets are highly complex systems that are influenced by human behavior and economic events. For decades, data scientists and economists have attempted to predict and explain financial time series, but it still remains a big challenge. Traditionally, autoregressive models or technical indicators were used to achieve this goal. While this approach provided a mathematical and statistical foundation, it falls short when confronted with unstructured data like news headlines or investor sentiment.

With the rapid advances in artificial intelligence (AI), especially in natural language processing (NLP), new ways of understanding unstructured text have been found. For example, transformer models can extract semantic information from news headlines. This information could be used to engineer new features that help explain variance in the movement of stock market indices like the S\&P500 or the MSCI World.

At the heart of this research lies the \textbf{Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)}, which states that market prices account for all available information nearly instantaneously. To explore this dynamic, this thesis investigates whether stochastic forecasting can be improved by integrating a sentiment indicator. This metric quantifies investor emotions by extracting data from news headlines and scaling it from -1 (bearish) to 1 (bullish). Applying modern sentiment analysis with a comprehensive evaluation across multiple models will yield a new data point in the ongoing discussion on whether the weak and/or semi-strong forms of this hypothesis holds true for the experiments of this work.

For analysing the quantitative benefit of this feature multiple traditional and complex machine learning models will be compared against baselines in regression and classification tasks. The evaluation is going to be performed by adding and removing the sentiment column from a given feature matrix to see if the prediction performance increases, flattens or decreases. To validate the outcome expanding window cross validation is used. Lastly to analyse the performance of both tasks common metrics like Mean Percentage Error (MAP) or F1-Scores are utilized.

\chapter{Literature Review}
At the core of financial market prediction lies the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) \cite{fama1965behavior}, which states that all available information is immediately priced in. Resulting in an environment in which consistent outperformance by leveraging current and past information is not possible. Specifically, this model can be broken down into three forms. The weak form includes past price data, while the semi-strong form also includes all other publicly available information such as financial news headlines. The strong form states that all information, whether public or private knowledge, is also immediately priced in \cite{emh1970}. While this model is a reasonable foundation, some other researchers see it highly critical. For example, the EMH assumes that investors are completely rational and can comprehend all information, which is unrealistic \cite{sul2017trading}. A good example of this point is provided by \cite{lawrence1997using}, which states that the market crash of 1987 was due to overwhelming investor fear rather than rational informed decisions. An alternative market model called Gradual Information Flow (GIF) was introduced in \cite{hong1999unified}. In short, the GIF model can be explained as categorizing investors into two groups. One group, the "news watchers", only focuses on fundamental news, and the other group, the "momentum traders", focuses only on past price data, with both failing to see the whole picture. This results in market anomalies where prices are not always reasonable. In the context of the GIF model, \cite{sul2017trading} notes that the rate at which information is consumed by the market is proportional to its exposure. For instance, a news headline published on a niche portal will not receive the same level of investor attention as an article from a famous financial source.

To capture the chaotic price changes and behavioral anomalies, researchers have shifted from traditional technical indicators often seen as subjective and unreliable \cite{lawrence1997using} to Neural Networks (NNs). Neural networks can help to analyze patterns behind the chaotic movement of the stock market \cite{ZHANG201860} or to analyze unstructured information like financial news. Especially with the advances in Natural Language Processing (NLP), there are some new opportunities for analyzing textual data. New models like FinBERT \cite{araci2019finbert} allow for a more sophisticated sentiment analysis as opposed to the traditional ways with lexicons \cite{loughran2011liability}. One empirical study \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742} utilized both financial news and social media data to gauge this sentiment. Combining it with technical indicators yielded improved performance on forex and crypto prices. Similarly, social media platforms like Twitter and StockTwits have been mined for real-time mood indicators improving prediction accuracy for major tech stocks by capturing same-day sentiment scores \cite{9175549,sul2017trading}. In predicting market direction (up, down), \cite{CHOI2024121589,khan2022stock} have used sentiment features to achieve a stated prediction accuracy of around 80\% with advanced ensemble methods like Random Forest or Gradient Boosting.

Despite the high performance claims, the ability to consistently and profitably beat the EMH remains an ongoing debate. \cite{ZHANG201860} argues that there is no guarantee that sentiment does not include noise and misleading information. This skepticism is also supported by \cite{jrfm18080412}, who analyzed 1.86 million headlines using multiple sentiment models (VADER, TextBlob, FinBERT). They concluded that explicit sentiment scores lack robust predictive power in regression and classification tasks, failing to reject the Efficient Market Hypothesis.

This ongoing debate highlights the following important question: where is sentiment actually effective? While previous studies \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742,9175549} show benefit on single equities or specific currency pairs, the prediction of broad market indices remains a unique challenge. This paper will focus on predicting the S\&P 500 index to test if sentiment can effectively reflect broad macroeconomic moods or if it merely introduces noise. By doing so, this study adds a new data point to the EMH and GIF discussion, testing the limits and boundaries of market efficiency.

\section{Sentiment Models}
For the sentiment classification task, two different models are used and compared: FinBERT and VADER. The reason for this decision is to compare which of the models performs better in sentiment analysis. Doing so will show how important contextual understanding of transformers is when it comes to sentiment classification of short texts like news headlines.

\subsection{VADER}
VADER is a traditional lexicon- and rule-based sentiment model \cite{hutto2014vader}. It was developed to analyze short sentences like social media posts, product reviews, or short editorials in newspapers. It assigns each word a predefined polarity score and adjusts the values based on heuristic rules. Negations, intensifiers (extremely, marginally, ...), and capitalization are some examples of these rules. They are used to gain more contextual understanding.

The final output of the model is called the compound score and is calculated by summing all polarity values and normalizing them:

$$s_{compound} = \frac{\sum v_i}{\sqrt{\left(\sum v_i\right)^2 + \alpha}}$$

Where $v_i$ are the adjusted polarity scores and $\alpha$ is a normalization constant (default value $\alpha = 15$), which acts as a sensitivity dial. As the final output, this formula guarantees a value between $-1$ (negative sentiment) and $1$ (positive sentiment).

\subsection{FinBERT}
FinBERT is a state-of-the-art sentiment model which, in contrast, uses a transformer architecture instead of a lexicon approach \cite{araci2019finbert}. It is a fine-tuned version of the famous BERT model from Google \cite{devlin2019bert} and was trained on a large corpus of financial text. Because of its architecture and fine-tuning, it excels at the task of understanding semantic text in the financial domain. At the end of the model architecture, a dense layer for classification was added. The output is passed through a softmax function to obtain the probability distribution of the three sentiment labels (positive, negative, and neutral).

$$P(y|x) = \text{softmax}(W \cdot h_{\text{[CLS]}} + b)$$

Where $P(y|x)$ stands for the conditional probability, $h_{\text{[CLS]}}$ for the CLS token (compressed representation vector of the sentence), and $W$ and $b$ for the learned parameters of the classification layer.

\section{Impact Scoring}
Financial markets create a large volume of textual data. However, the distribution of important information is highly skewed. Most headlines are "noise" (repetitive updates, foreign news, minor price fluctuations), which can dilute the predictive power of the sentiment score. For this reason, a novel denoising method that uses large language models (LLMs) to differentiate between important headlines and noise is introduced.

The main component of this filtering process is Llama 3.1 Instruct (8B). To increase throughput and memory efficiency, the model was implemented using 6-bit quantization (.gguf format). The resulting memory footprint is approximately 6.85 GB, which enables local inference on consumer-grade hardware. Because this model is not fine-tuned, a prompt had to be engineered. Headlines are categorized into four classes (A, B, C, D) based on their predictive power for future S&P 500 returns. These labels are then transformed into a numerical value ($A \rightarrow 1$, $B \rightarrow 0.66$, $C \rightarrow 0.33$, $D \rightarrow 0$) called the impact score.

The final output of this procedure is a denoised, weighted sentiment score. Rather than treating every sentiment score equally, we apply the impact score as an additional transformation. This ensures that a positive sentiment for a minor headline (Category C) contributes less to the overall trend than a positive sentiment for a major headline (Category A). The calculation is defined as:

$$S_{w} = S \times I$$

Where $S_w$ is the final weighted sentiment, \\
$S$ is the raw sentiment score (ranging from $-1$ to $1$), and \\
$I$ is the LLM-derived impact score (ranging from $0$ to $1$).

This approach will demonstrate how well a model can learn to predict not only the direction of the price change but also its magnitude.
The target variable for the regression models is the percentage change of S\&P 500 returns at time $t+1$.

$$R_t = \frac{P_t - P_{t-1}}{P_{t-1}}$$

To evaluate the effectiveness of each forecaster, a baseline model was implemented that simply predicts the mean price change of the training set. This serves as a benchmark to determine whether the inclusion of sentiment features explains additional variance in the target variable.

$$\bar{y} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i$$
To compare the forecasting models across various feature sets, the following loss function and metrics are utilized:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean Squared Error (MSE)}: While used as a loss function for regression models like multiple linear regression (MLR), for the final evaluation pipeline, MSE is excluded because it is not suited for small numbers like percentage changes. Instead, Mean Absolute Error and Root Mean Squared Error are used for the final comparison.
    \item \textbf{Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)}: Helps to transform the magnitude of the error back into the units of the target variable, making it easier to interpret.
    \item \textbf{Mean Absolute Error (MAE)}: In contrast to MSE and RMSE, it does not square errors, making it less sensitive to outliers.
    \item \textbf{R-squared ($R^2$)}: Measures the proportion of variance in the target variable that can be explained by the feature set. If the value is around 0, it means that the model is no better than the baseline (always predicting the mean of the train target distribution).
\end{itemize}

To achieve a robust analysis of the regression task, a mix of traditional and advanced machine learning models is used. Using both statistical baselines and more complex recurrent neural networks provides a better comparison than relying on a single architecture. Therefore, the following models will be evaluated:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{ARMA(X) (Auto-Regressive Moving Average)}: A statistical model for forecasting time series data. It combines an Auto-Regressive (AR) component, which uses previous target variables, and a Moving Average (MA) component, which uses the model's past forecasting errors. To include additional inputs like sentiment, an extended version called ARMAX is used (the X stands for eXogenous variables).
\item \textbf{LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)}: An advanced RNN (Recurrent Neural Network) architecture that uses three distinct gates (input, output, and forget) to improve long-term dependency learning. In financial forecasting, LSTM is frequently used and is among the most performant models. %Quellen hier zitieren
\item \textbf{GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit)}: A type of RNN designed to efficiently handle sequential data. Compared to the LSTM model, it has only two gates (update and reset) to mitigate the vanishing gradient problem. Because of its architecture, it is more lightweight than an LSTM but often achieves comparable performance.
\item \textbf{MLR (Multiple Linear Regression)}: A fundamental statistical method that models the linear relationship between the target variable and two or more explanatory features. In this evaluation, MLR serves as a highly interpretable, linear baseline model to contrast against the more complex, non-linear deep learning approaches (GRU and LSTM).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Classification}
\subsection{Classification}
In contrast to the regression task, the classification task shifts the focus away from predicting exact magnitudes to forecasting the binary direction of market movements. Specifically, this evaluation will analyze whether the sentiment feature is beneficial in accurately classifying the direction of the market (up or down).

The target variable for the classification task is the binary direction of the market, $Y_{t+1}$. Specifically, it indicates whether the S\&P 500 percentage change (as defined in the regression section) is positive or flat/negative. The formula is as follows:

$$Y_{t+1} = \begin{cases} 1, & \text{if } R_{t+1} > 0 \\ 0, & \text{if } R_{t+1} \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

A possible baseline for this task would be a random guessing model that predicts "up" and "down" with equal probability. However, historical market data reveals a class imbalance in favor of upward movements. Therefore, the baseline will be a classification model that consistently predicts the majority class, providing a more robust and realistic benchmark for comparison.

To evaluate the performance and compare classification models across different feature sets, the following metrics are utilized:
\begin{itemize}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accuracy}: While accuracy is an important benchmark for understanding model performance, it does not always provide a complete picture. A model could learn to always predict "up" and yield a seemingly good performance, even if it is not optimal. For this reason, other metrics derived from the confusion matrix are necessary.
    \item \textbf{Precision}: In the context of financial forecasting, precision is critical because it indicates how reliable a model is when it signals an upward market movement. This metric is important if the goal is to minimize false buy signals.
    \item \textbf{Recall}: A model with high recall (sensitivity) misses very few upward cases (investment opportunities).
    \item \textbf{F1-Score}: A metric that provides a comprehensive overview of the trade-off between precision and recall. Specifically, it highlights the balance between successfully identifying all positive cases and actually being correct with those positive predictions.
\end{itemize}

To conduct a robust evaluation of the classification task, a traditional statistical method is compared against an advanced ensemble learning technique. The following models will be evaluated:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multiple Logistic Regression (MLogR)}: This model is the classification counterpart to Multiple Linear Regression. It estimates the probability of an upward market movement using a logistic function based on the explanatory features. In this evaluation, MLogR serves as a highly interpretable, linear baseline, providing a benchmark to analyze whether more complex, non-linear relationships exist within the data.
    \item \textbf{XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting)}: This is a highly performant, tree-based ensemble learning algorithm. It is robust to outliers, handles non-linear relationships effectively, and is widely regarded as a state-of-the-art model for classifying tabular financial data.
\end{itemize}

\section{Validation}
Because financial forecasting is based on timeseries based market data we cannot use normal cross validation like k-fold. The reason is that if we shuffle datapoints randomly we break the causality of time (yesterday can not happend before tomorrow). Therefore a different approach hast to be used. There are two main ways on how to cross validate the evaluation models:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rolling Window}: In this approach the size of the traing data is fixed. After finishing a validation step, new data becomes part of the next train set while the old data is droped. This leads to a sliding window over the whole data with multiple combinations of train and test sets.
    \item \textbf{Expanding Window}: In this appraoch the size of the training data is growing with each validation step. After each round new obeservations are included in the train set. Therefore the model learns from the maximum available historical context.
\end{itemize}

In this work the Expanding Window strategy is used to incooperate the whole train set in the hope that the model can achieve better generalization. Because this paper evaluates varying forecast horizons, the standard implementation of expanding window was adapted to prevent look-ahead bias. Specifically, days that are used to calculate the target are excluded by a gap between train and test set. For example, if the target is a 3-day percentage change utilizing $y_{t-1}$, $y_{t-2}$, and $y_{t-3}$. Non of 3 days may be included in the train set. This assures a strict boundary for the models learning data, with no information leaks into the future.

\section{Impact Score}
In this section the effectivesness of the novel denoising function of this work will be analysed. In the world of stock market prediction, distinguishing between meaningful and noise in financial headlines is not trivial. For example the news headline "Buy these 10 stocks today" has near to zero use for future prediction while "the us fed increasing interest rate by 0.5 percentage" will have a bigger and more pronounced impact on the market. To filter relevant news from irrelevant impact scoring is utilized, by using modern large language models to classify datapoints into 4 categories (A -> very impactful, D -> noise).

The scale of this denoising process was substantial, because the dataset consisted of a massive raw pool of 1.9 million financial headlines and computational resources where limited. Therefore to filter most noise out one important preprocessing was to filter duplicated rows. The assumption is that news that repreat will likely be noise. With this rule the amount of headlines shrink by an astonishing 1 Million datapoints- showing the amount of redundant news headlines. Before diving into the specific experiment setup, it is important to adress the compautation cost for this experiment. The classification process was run on a RTX 5070ti hosting the LLama 3.1 8B Instruct model. With an average 13 headliens per seconds and 919.858 classifications the total time needed resulted in 19 hours. This shows that even with limited computational power a broad headline corpus can be processed in reasonable time. To improve processing efficiency 6-bit GGUF quantization is utilized, a choice that significantly lowers memory usage and accelerates inference speed. To ensure the model’s output could be cleanly parsed into specific classes, the LLM was prompted to return results only in a valid JSON format. Because large language models can behave unexpectedly, a gracefull error handling was essential. Therefore, a retry mechanism was implemented to re-execute the process whenever an invalid response is received. In the case of the 3 failed attempts the headlines is excluded by setting its impact score to NaN.

\section{Different Target Horinzons}
% explain the last modification idea for the basic experiment: using longer prediction horizons
% Speculate on the idea why next day prediction is chaotic and volatile and hard for machine learning models to predict. And how fluctuations flattens out when looked at a longer time horizon
% present the concrete modifications of the basic experiment 1d, 3d, 5d, 20d.
The final modification for the basic experiment involves changing the prediction target. In this section, the forcasting performance is tested across multiple target horizons. The fundamental idea is that next day stock preidction is extremly difficult due to the volatile and chaotic fluctuation of market prices. This high variance makes it very challenging for machine learning algorithms to extract meaningful patterns, even when provided with high-quality predictors. By extending the target timeframe, the focus shifts from short-term noise to broader market trends. Consequently, the underlying assumption is that price changes smooth out over longer periods, reducing overall volatility and enabling the evaluation models to learn fundamental relationships. Precisely, the basic experiment will be changed to accomodate three new target horizons: 3-days, 5-days and 20-days.

\chapter{Discussion}
The primary objective of this research was to evaluate the benefit of sentiment analysis on financial news headlines for predicting the S\&P500 index. Across a vast series of experiments (utilizing different sentiment models, sentiment feature engineering, noise reduction techniques, and different target horizons) a consistent picture emerged. The engineerd sentiment feature captures meaningful market dynamics. However, it is not enough to beat naive baselines in a fair, no lookahead setup. Despite utilizing state of the art transformer-based sentiment models (FinBERT), classical lexicon-based models (VADER) and a novel impact scoring mechanism based on LLMs, none of the evaluation models successfully outperformed baselines in regression and classification tasks.
The most critical takeaway from the experiment is the efficiency of the modern stock market. The missing correlation between sentiment and next-day price change suggests that the information contained in news is priced into the market in a very short time. This is further supported by the experiment where lookahead bias (same-day sentiment) was intentionally introduced. The result showed that there is a significant correlation with the target and the evaluation models did outperform the baselines. This confirms that sentiment scores are not merely noise but contain explanatory power regarding market movements. However, the lifetime of this signal seems to be very short, suggesting that the semi-strong form of the efficient market hypothesis holds for all performed experiments.
Results might differ in other setups. For example \cite{ANBAEEFARIMANI2022108742} showed that with hourly sentiment a positive correlation to the target is achievable. In addtion as stated by the GIF (Gradient Information Flow) price change of less exposed targets (like small or niche companies) or less popular news sources might experience a longer lived sentiment, because information is not consumed as fast as compared to efficient economic markets like the S\&P500.
Another notable aspect that has been adressed in this work is the denoising of the input data. Financial news and especially news headlines can be redundant and needs filtering. While there are possible rule based filtering methods modern approaches like using LLMs introduce new ways on how this problem can be solved. One of the only basic experiment adjustment that brought consistent improvement was the impact score. This shows some promising potential for LLMs to act as semantic filters, distinguishing between trivial market noise and events with real economic significance.

\chapter{Future Work}
While this researche´s findings show clear limitations, the fundamental ideas can be a starting point for further investigations.

\section{Utilizing of Fine-Granulated Sentiment Data}
Since the semi-strong form of the EMH appears to hold for daily aggregated sentiment, future research should transition toward intraday or hourly sentiment aggregation on the S\%P500. While this data is more expensive and harder to find it could introduce a very important benefit: using higher frequencies, predicitve models might be able to capture information that is not fully priced in yet. This would result in a positive correlation with the target and potential explainatory power of the sentiment feature.

\section{Denoising with STOA- Fine-Tuned- LLMs}
In this work a lightweight model was utilized to perform impact scoring. More resources would allow for more sophisticated denoising mechanisms. leveraging state of the art models like Gemini Pro 3.1 or GPT 5.5 could inhance the performance of understanding financial nuances, improving denosing, and simultaneously reduce the dependency on prompt engineering. Alternativly, performance could also optimized by creating a fine-tuned model that was trained to classify financial text in relevant or irrelevant. This would allow to achieve good impact scoring performance while keeping the needed computational requirements low.

\section{Sentiment Score as Embedding}
A fundamental limitation of sentiment analysis with FinBERT or similar models, which classify datapoints in a 3-dimensional vector (positive, netural, negative), is the information loss that comes with the reduction of dimensionality. A headline about a devastating corporate lawsuit and a headline about a minor earnings miss yield the same negative sentiment score. Future research could leverage high dimensional text embeddings to preserve semantic information. However, to support such a new feature, future pipelines face other challenges, like aggregating scores over multiple headlines or processing high-dimensional features.

\chapter{Conclusion}
The fundamental question that this study was trying to answer is "Can daily aggregated sentiment extrated from financial news headlines improve prediction accuracy in financial forcasting on the S\&P500?". This research evaluated the predictive power of such feature accross multiple evaluation models in regression and classification tasks. Through a robust series of experiments utilizing transformer-based architectures (FinBERT), lexicon-based models (VADER), varying target horizons, and denosing by impact scoring, a consistent conclusion can be made: daily aggreagted sentiment fails to systematically outperform any naive baseline models in regression and classification tasks. This work therefore fails to reject the semi-strong form of the efficient market hypothesis, suggesting that daily aggregated sentiment alone is not enough to improve predicitve capabilities. However, with the introduction of LLM-based filtering a promising way for data denoising has been evaluated. Even though the resulting gains were not yet sufficient to overcome the robust baselines it suggests that semantic filtering has greate potential to extract clearer and stronger sentiment signals.
